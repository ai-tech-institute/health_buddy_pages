# Chronic Migraine: Understanding Migraine Phases

For those newly diagnosed with chronic migraine, understanding the distinct phases of a migraine attack is crucial for better management and control. Migraine is a complex neurological event, typically unfolding in four stages: Prodrome, Aura, Attack, and Postdrome. Recognizing these phases empowers patients to anticipate attacks, initiate early treatments, and understand their unique patterns.

## 1. Prodrome (The Warning Phase)
Often overlooked, the prodrome phase can begin hours or days before headache pain. This "pre-headache" stage affects 70-80% of migraineurs, with diverse symptoms like mood changes, fatigue, or neck stiffness. Learning to identify these subtle cues is invaluable. Keep a detailed migraine diary to reveal personal prodromal triggers. Early intervention during prodrome, such as taking prescribed acute medications like NSAIDs or triptans *as directed by your doctor*, can sometimes abort an attack or significantly reduce its severity.

## 2. Aura (The Sensory Disturbance)
The aura phase affects 25-30% of sufferers, typically occurring just before or during the headache. It features transient neurological symptoms, most commonly visual disturbances (flashing lights, zig-zag lines, blind spots). Less common are sensory changes or speech difficulties. Aura symptoms are usually reversible, lasting 5 to 60 minutes. *Some individuals may experience migraine with aura without a headache, known as 'silent migraine.'* If you experience new or unusual neurological symptoms, especially without a preceding migraine history, consult your doctor to rule out other conditions.

## 3. Attack (The Headache Phase)
This is the most debilitating phase, characterized by moderate to severe throbbing pain, often on one side. Accompanying symptoms include nausea, vomiting, and sensitivity to light, sound, or smell. Physical activity can worsen pain. The attack can last 4 to 72 hours. For chronic migraine, attacks occur 15+ days/month. *Taking acute medication as early as possible offers the best chance for efficacy.* Common acute treatments include:
*   **Triptans (e.g., Sumatriptan):** Constrict blood vessels, block pain pathways. FDA-approved for acute migraine. Not for those with certain heart conditions or uncontrolled blood pressure. Side effects: chest tightness, tingling, dizziness.
*   **Oral CGRP Inhibitors (e.g., Ubrelvy, Nurtec ODT):** Block CGRP receptors. FDA-approved for acute migraine (Nurtec ODT also for prevention). Suitable if triptans are unsuitable. Side effects: nausea, drowsiness.
*   **NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen):** Reduce inflammation and pain. Available OTC/prescription. Use with caution for stomach/kidney concerns. Side effect: stomach upset.
*   **Medication Overuse Headache (MOH):** Crucially, frequent use of acute medications (more than 2-3 days/week) can paradoxically worsen headaches. Discuss this risk and medication frequency with your doctor.

## 4. Postdrome (The Migraine Hangover)
Often described as a "migraine hangover," this phase follows the attack, lasting hours to a full day. Symptoms include fatigue, difficulty concentrating, neck pain, and a "drained" feeling. Rest, hydration, and avoiding triggers can aid recovery. *Specifically track postdrome symptoms and duration in your migraine diary; this helps anticipate recovery time and plan activities, improving quality of life.*

Understanding these phases empowers newly diagnosed patients to proactively manage their condition, communicate effectively with healthcare providers, and ultimately improve their quality of life.
