# Chronic Migraine: Preventing Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)

For those newly diagnosed with chronic migraine, preventing Medication Overuse Headache (MOH) is crucial. Also called "rebound headache," MOH occurs when acute pain relievers are used too often, paradoxically worsening headaches. Early recognition is vital for long-term management.

### Understanding the Risk

MOH can develop with almost any acute headache medication: OTC pain relievers (ibuprofen, acetaminophen), prescription triptans (sumatriptan), and caffeine-containing medications (Excedrin Migraine). Risk depends on frequency. Remember the "10-day rule" for triptans/combination analgesics, and "15-day rule" for simple analgesics, per month. Exceeding these limits increases MOH risk. Track medication use meticulously.

### Practical Steps for Prevention

1.  **Medication Tracking:** Maintain a detailed headache diary: date, severity, medication, dosage, frequency. Apps like Migraine Buddy help. Your diary identifies patterns. Consistently nearing/exceeding 10/15-day limits, or decreased acute medication effectiveness, are red flags. Discuss these immediately with your provider.

2.  **Establish a Comprehensive Treatment Plan:** Work with your provider, ideally a **migraine or headache specialist** for complex cases, on a comprehensive plan including acute and preventive therapies.
    *   **Oral Preventives:** Topiramate (tingling, cognitive slowing possible) or propranolol (fatigue, dizziness possible) reduce migraine frequency.
    *   **Advanced Preventives (FDA-approved for chronic migraine):** Often considered after oral options.
        *   **CGRP Monoclonal Antibodies (e.g., Aimovig, Ajovy):** Self-injected monthly/quarterly or IV (Vyepti). Mild side effects like injection site reactions.
        *   **Botox Injections:** Doctor-administered every 12 weeks for 15+ headache days/month. Potential for neck pain.
    These preventives reduce acute medication reliance and MOH risk.

3.  **Recognize Warning Signs:** Be aware of increasing headache frequency or changing patterns despite acute medications. This signals developing MOH. If acute medications become less effective or are needed more often, consult your doctor immediately.

4.  **Avoid Certain Medications:** Opioids and barbiturate-containing medications (e.g., Fioricet) carry higher MOH/dependence risk; long-term use is discouraged. Be cautious with OTCs containing caffeine (e.g., Excedrin Migraine); frequent use contributes to MOH via caffeine withdrawal. Discuss safer alternatives with your doctor.

5.  **Non-Pharmacological Strategies:** Integrate non-drug approaches: stress management (mindfulness), regular exercise, consistent sleep, and identifying/avoiding personal migraine triggers. These reduce overall migraine burden and lessen reliance on acute medications.

Preventing MOH requires vigilance and proactive management. Understanding risks, tracking medication, and collaborating with your healthcare team improves long-term migraine outcomes and quality of life.
